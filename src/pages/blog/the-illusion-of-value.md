Lately, I’ve been thinking a lot about the UK supermarket “Christmas Price War”, where major retailers slash the prices of festive essentials to as little as 5p during the Christmas season.

But despite nearly every major supermarket participating in this annual battle for consumers, not all retailers cut prices equally. Some continue charging noticeably more than their competitors, yet still manage to attract huge numbers of shoppers throughout the festive period.

That raised an interesting question for me: if consumers know cheaper options exist, why do they still shop at more expensive supermarkets during the Christmas price war?

## Loss Leaders

If you aren't from the UK, you may be wondering what the "Christmas Price War" actually is. In a nutshell, every year in the weeks coming up to Christmas, major supermarkets begin aggressively lowering their prices on festive esstentials (mostly the same vegetables across the board), sometimes pricing them as low as 5p during the 2025 festive season, and occasionally even giving products away for free.

On the surface, this probably sounds irrational. Selling such common products for pennies seems like a guarenteed way to lose money - and it is! The supermarkets have no intention to make a profit from the vegetables themselves, but instead use them to function as loss leaders.

In more economical terms, a loss leader is a pricing strategy where a product is sold at a price below its market cost to stimulate other sales of more profitable goods or services. In this case, vegetables like carrots and potatoes act as a popular article to use for sales promotions sold at a low price to attract customers.

## The Paradox of Cheapness

Almost all of the mainstream supermarkets participate in the same price war, but not all retailers price their products equally. Discount supermarkets such as Aldi or Lidl tend to push their prices to the lowest extremes, while supermarkets like Waitrose and Sainsbury's maintain comparatively higher prices (at around 30p) for mostly the same festive range.

If customers were purely rational economic actors, the cheapest supermarkets should dominate Christmas shopping entirely. But that rarely happens. Despite varying prices, and many discounts being locked behind the respective retailers loyalty card schemes, supermarkets across the board retain their customers.

Clearly, people are not simply shopping for the lowest prices, and the more expensive supermarkets in this war are banking on that, otherwise shoppers would gravitate entirely towards the absolute cheapest prices, completely eliminating the point of a loss leader.

## The Halo Effect

The supermarkets understand this as fact. The purpose of the loss leader is not to sell vegetables. The purpose is to change consumer perception of the entire store.

Many of the discounted products have restrictions: loyalty card requirements, minimum spend thresholds, and anti-stockpiling policies to limit the number of items a customer can purchase. On the surface, this seems contradictory. If supermarkets truly wanted to compete on price alone, restricting access to the cheapest products would make little sense.

But these items are not designed to be profitable on their own. Instead, they function as strategically underpriced products intended to draw consumers into stores and encourage larger overall spending.

The positive impression created by these promotions is often far more valuable than the affordability of the products themselves. This is sometimes descrbed as the halo effect, where a customers associates the entire supermarket with affordability even if most other products are more expensive than competitors.

## Beyond the Price

Learning about the halo effect helped me tackle the question I had in the beginning. Customers still shop at the more expensive retailers because of the perceived value, familiarity, and convenience attached to shopping itself. They aren't always responding to objective prices.

Christmas shopping is often stressful, crowded, and time-sensitive. For many consumers, the ability to complete a large shop in a familiar environment is more valuable than marginal savings spread across individual items. If they've been shopping at one supermarket for years, the predictability and convinience can easily outweigh the marginal price benefits offered elsewhere.

In this sense, the “Christmas Price War” is not really a competition over vegetables at all. It is a competition over perception. Retailers do not need to slash their prices to the most extreme levels to remain part of the conversation; they simply need to convince consumers that their supermarket offers good overall value.

It may initially appear irrational for supermarkets to aggressively discount products, but the economic strategies are far more pyschological than they first appear.

In the end, the “Christmas Price War” reveals something larger about consumer behaviour itself: people rarely buy based on price alone. More often, we buy based on how value is perceived. That's why it works.
